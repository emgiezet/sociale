Nobody talks about the emotional cost of leading a team through AI transformation.

I lead an 11-person AI engineering team at Insly. The past 12 months have been the most technically interesting of my 20-year career. They've also been some of the most exhausting.

Not because of the code. Because of the people.

There's an anxiety that sits quietly under the surface of a high-performing engineering team when the ground starts shifting. Engineers who were top performers in one paradigm start questioning whether their skills still matter. They don't always say it out loud. You feel it in the questions they ask, the ones they stop asking, and the moments when someone goes quiet in a meeting where they used to speak up.

I've felt it myself. Some mornings I open my laptop genuinely unsure whether the architectural decision I made three months ago was the right one — or whether I shipped a system that will need to be rebuilt in a year. That uncertainty doesn't go away. You learn to work with it.

The pressure from leadership is different in kind. They want progress, timelines, ROI. AI doesn't produce clean timelines. Research phases look like nothing happening. A spike that teaches you what won't work feels like failure to someone reading a status report.

What I've learned to do: name the uncertainty explicitly. With my team, with leadership. "We don't know yet" said clearly is better than a confident answer that turns out to be wrong six weeks later.

I've also learned to celebrate the negative results. The system that didn't ship because we caught the evaluation failure early is a win. It rarely feels like one in the moment.

**Transformations don't happen to teams — they happen through them. The emotional dimension is not separate from the technical work. It is the work.**
