Symfony is 20 years old. Here's why it's still the backbone of enterprise PHP — and what AI changes.

I wrote my first Symfony application in the early 2010s. I've contributed to SonataAdminBundle, which now has 18 million installs. I've spent 15 years watching Symfony projects succeed in production where other frameworks failed. The anniversary is worth marking — and worth thinking about honestly.

Symfony earned its longevity the hard way:

→ Contracts over implementations. The service container and the component architecture meant you could swap pieces without rewriting the world. That's not a feature — it's a philosophy that aged well.

→ Stability guarantees. LTS releases with 3-year support horizons. Enterprise teams can't bet their roadmap on a framework that breaks its API every 18 months. Symfony understood this from early on.

→ Boring is a compliment. The framework doesn't chase trends. When GraphQL was everywhere, Symfony didn't pivot its core. When NoSQL was going to replace everything, Symfony kept its Doctrine integration clean and didn't panic. Enterprise software runs for decades.

Now: what does AI change for Symfony developers?

AI coding assistants are exceptional at Symfony. The codebase is large and well-documented, which means models have strong training signal. Generating service definitions, writing entity repositories, scaffolding controllers — these tasks that used to take an hour take minutes.

But here's the catch: AI-generated Symfony code will pass review if you don't know Symfony well. The generated service might be functionally correct but miss the container optimization that matters at scale. The generated controller might ignore the security voter pattern your codebase relies on.

AI doesn't replace Symfony expertise. It rewards it. The developers who get the most from AI assistance on Symfony are the ones who immediately spot when the generated code cuts the right corners versus the wrong ones.

**20 years of Symfony taught me that good architecture outlasts any tool. AI is the newest tool. The architecture principles aren't new.**
