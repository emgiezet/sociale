Last month I ran 360-degree feedback for my 11-person team. One response changed how I lead.

I'm a Tech Lead at Insly. We're an 11-person AI engineering team, moving fast in a domain (InsurTech) where mistakes have real consequences. I had been running the team for a while and thought I had a reasonable read on how things were going.

I was wrong about one thing 🫠

The feedback was mostly what I expected: communication could be clearer, more visibility into priorities, the usual items that come up in any fast-moving team. I was ready for those.

What I wasn't ready for was this (paraphrased): "Sometimes it feels like decisions are already made when we're asked for input."

That landed hard. Because I don't think of myself as someone who does that. I ask for opinions. I run refinements. I encourage pushback. But the perception existed. And perception is what you're actually managing.

I spent two weeks thinking about where the gap was. What I found: I was sharing my lean too early. I'd walk into a discussion with a preferred direction already half-formed, and even when I asked for input, my framing made the "right answer" obvious. People weren't lying when they gave feedback. They were reading the room correctly.

The change I made: I now separate the problem statement from the solution phase explicitly. Present the problem first. Close my mouth. Only offer my direction after others have spoken.

It sounds obvious. It wasn't obvious while I was doing it the wrong way 🤷‍♂️

**The best thing a 360 gives you isn't validation. It's the one thing you couldn't see because you were too close to it.**
