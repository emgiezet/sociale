The '10x developer' doesn't exist. But the 10x team does. Here's how I build one.

I've been leading engineers for years. I've met a handful of people who code unusually fast. I've never met one whose output alone moved a team from mediocre to exceptional. That's not how it works.

At Insly I run an 11-person AI engineering team. We ship production systems in a regulated industry with real consequences for errors. Here's what I've learned about building a team that actually multiplies output.

**Psychological safety first, velocity second.** Engineers who are afraid to look stupid don't experiment. And in AI, experimentation is the job. I explicitly reward "I tried this and it failed, here's what I learned" in retrospectives. It took months to normalize this. Worth every single month 💯

**Short, honest feedback loops beat long performance reviews.** We do quick weekly check-ins: what's blocked, what's confusing, what's frustrating. Not "how are you," but specific questions. Problems get caught in days, not quarters.

**The senior engineers' job is to shrink uncertainty for everyone else.** When we adopted AI tooling, the experienced engineers went first, documented what worked, and then taught it. Nobody was left to figure it out alone.

**Hire for learning speed, not current skills.** I've hired engineers who didn't know Python and became productive with AI tooling in weeks. The ones who resist learning compound that resistance over time 📉

One concrete result: when we adopted LightRAG for our second RAG system, the whole team was productive in it within two sprints. Because we had built the habit of learning together.

**A 10x team isn't 10 great developers. It's a team that makes every developer 10% better every month.**
