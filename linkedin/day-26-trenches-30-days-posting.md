## LinkedIn Post

I've posted on LinkedIn every weekday for a month.
Here's what surprised me.

I had expectations. Most were wrong.

**What I expected:**
→ Technical posts would get the most engagement
→ The first week would be the hardest
→ Follower count would be the main metric worth watching

**What actually happened:**

→ **The posts about leadership and team dynamics outperformed technical posts.** My post about the emotional cost of AI transformation got more engagement than any architecture deep-dive. People are hungry to talk about the human side of tech.

→ **Week 3 was the hardest, not Week 1.** Week 1 runs on novelty energy. Week 3, you've used your "obvious" ideas and now you have to dig. The posts I thought least original generated the best conversations.

→ **The comments are the content.** I've learned more from comment sections than from writing the posts. People share war stories, push back on my assumptions, and add context I didn't have. Two posts led to direct conversations that might turn into consulting relationships.

→ **Polish posts had disproportionate reach in the Polish tech community.** Smaller total engagement but significantly higher engagement rate from exactly the audience I wanted to reach.

→ **Consistency matters more than any single post.** I've seen the follower count grow, but the qualitative shift — people reaching out, mentioning specific posts, sharing content with their teams — started around day 15.

**The thing nobody tells you about posting consistently: it's a discipline that compounds. The 26th post is better than the first post, not because I'm a better writer, but because I've had 25 conversations that sharpened my thinking.**

## Blog Post

### What a Month of Daily LinkedIn Posts Taught Me About Building an Audience

I started this 30-day sprint with a straightforward premise: share what I know about building AI systems in production, consistently, every weekday, and see what compounded.

One month in, the results are more surprising than I expected — and the surprises are mostly about the nature of the audience, the format, and the relationship between consistency and quality.

#### What I Got Wrong Before I Started

**I thought technical depth would drive engagement.** I'm a technical person writing for technical people. My hypothesis: the more specific and detailed the technical content, the more engagement it would get.

Wrong. Some of my most technically detailed posts — the LightRAG vs. Bedrock comparison, the RAG evaluation framework — got solid engagement but not the highest. The posts about the emotional cost of leading through AI transformation and the nature of high-performing teams reached significantly more people and generated richer comment threads.

The lesson: technical depth is necessary for credibility. It's not sufficient for reach. Content that combines technical perspective with human experience — leadership, team dynamics, career evolution — reaches the intersection of audiences who care about both.

**I thought the first week would be hardest.** The first week runs on preparation energy and novelty motivation. You've been planning content, you have ideas queued up, the habit feels fresh.

Week 3 was when the internal critic got loudest: "This post isn't as good as the first five." "I'm repeating myself." "Who actually cares about this?" Week 3 is where the habit either becomes durable or breaks. The discipline of posting anyway — even when uncertain, even when the content feels ordinary — is what separates sustained effort from a burst.

**I thought follower count was the metric.** Followers are a lagging indicator. The leading indicators that actually changed my understanding of progress: direct messages from people who wanted to discuss specific posts, shares by people whose judgment I respect, comment threads where the participants taught me something I hadn't known.

These qualitative signals started appearing around day 15, which tracks with the idea that audiences need pattern-matching time — they need to see enough consistency to understand what you stand for before they invest in following closely.

#### The Comment Section as Research

The most underrated benefit of consistent posting: the comment sections are an astonishing research tool.

My post about "just add AI" dynamics generated comments from developers in banking, healthcare, legal, and manufacturing who were facing the same pressure with the same stakeholder dynamics. The specific details varied; the structural pattern was identical.

My post about vibecoding and developer judgment sparked a long thread from experienced developers who had very different views on where the line should be drawn. I came away with four perspectives I hadn't considered.

My post about leading through AI transformation generated DMs from people in leadership roles who were relieved to see the emotional dimension named explicitly. Most of them said it was content they'd been looking for and not finding.

This feedback loop — posting something that prompts a range of responses, which refines my understanding of the question, which improves the next post — is the actual value of consistent posting. The content gets better because you're having 30 conversations you wouldn't otherwise have.

#### Polish Content in a Global Platform

A specific finding worth sharing: posting in Polish on a primarily English platform generates a different engagement profile than posting in English.

Polish posts have lower total engagement numbers but dramatically higher engagement from the specific audience I care most about reaching — Polish technology leaders, AI practitioners, and developers in regulated industries in Poland. The signal-to-noise ratio is higher.

This suggests a general principle: language is a filter as well as a reach mechanism. If your primary goal is reaching a specific community rather than maximizing absolute reach, the language choice is a tool worth using intentionally.

I post approximately 30% of my content in Polish, consistent with my goal of building depth in the Polish tech community alongside international reach.

#### What Actually Changed in 30 Days

The external metrics: follower count up, reach significantly higher, inbound messages more frequent.

The more interesting changes: my thinking about AI in production has sharpened. Writing about something forces you to articulate it with a precision that's different from understanding it internally. Posting about RAG evaluation for 30 days has made me a better evaluator. Writing about the emotional cost of AI transformation made me more attentive to it in my own team.

I didn't expect that writing for an external audience would improve my internal thinking as much as it has. But it makes sense: the discipline of making ideas legible to strangers is a high-leverage form of clarity-building.

#### Whether You Should Start

If you're a technical person with real experience who's been waiting for a "good time" to start sharing consistently on LinkedIn: the good time is now. Not because the platform is optimal or the algorithm is favorable. Because the discipline of consistent, specific communication about what you actually know is valuable regardless of audience size.

Start with one post per week if five is too ambitious. Start with a subject you know well enough to write about without research. Start with what's true from your experience, not what you think the audience wants to hear.

The 26th post will be better than the first because of the 25 conversations you'll have had. The only way to have those conversations is to start.
