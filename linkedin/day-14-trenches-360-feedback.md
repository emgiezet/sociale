## LinkedIn Post

Last month I ran 360-degree feedback for my 11-person team.
The results were uncomfortable. That was the point.

I've led engineering teams for over a decade. I've done performance reviews, 1:1s, retrospectives. But I'd never run a full 360 — collecting structured feedback on each person from their manager, peers, and reports.

What I learned:

→ **The feedback that surprised people most wasn't negative. It was positive.** Several engineers didn't know that their teammates specifically valued them for things they considered "minor" — communication style, quality of code reviews, how they handle interruptions. Knowing that something you take for granted is valuable to others is motivating in a different way than a bonus.

→ **The gaps between self-perception and peer perception were the most useful data.** One engineer rated themselves low on communication. Their peers rated them high. That misalignment is worth an entire 1:1. Another rated themselves high on technical depth. Peers were more mixed. Also worth an entire 1:1.

→ **Leading a team through AI transformation adds a layer of uncertainty that traditional performance feedback doesn't capture.** "How are they performing?" is harder to answer when the benchmarks are moving weekly. I had to be honest with my team about that uncertainty.

→ **It's not enough to collect feedback. You have to tell people what you found.** I shared every team member's individual summary with them in person, with context, not just a report dropped in their inbox.

The process took weeks and was genuinely difficult. I'd do it again.

**If you lead a team and you're waiting for a "good time" to do 360 feedback: there isn't one. That's not a reason to skip it.**

Have you run 360 feedback on your team? What surprised you?

## Blog Post

### What Running 360-Degree Feedback on an 11-Person Team Actually Taught Me

There's a version of 360-degree feedback that's a bureaucratic exercise: forms go out, forms come back, HR tallies the scores, managers share a sanitized summary with each employee, and everyone returns to their normal work having learned approximately nothing.

That's not what I did. And the gap between that version and what actually happened is worth writing about.

#### Why I Did It

I lead an 11-person engineering team at Insly. We've been in the middle of an AI transformation for the past 18 months — learning new technologies, building new systems, developing new skills under production pressure. It's been stimulating and, for some team members, genuinely stressful.

I'd been doing regular 1:1s, quarterly retrospectives, and informal check-ins. But I had a feeling — not data, a feeling — that I was missing something. That the informal feedback channels weren't surfacing everything that was there to surface.

360-degree feedback is structured specifically to surface things that informal channels miss: how peers genuinely perceive each other's work, how team members experience being managed, what skills each person brings that others depend on but might never explicitly name.

I decided to do it properly: structured questionnaires for each person, covering behavioral dimensions (communication, technical quality, collaboration, reliability, growth) rather than abstract ratings. Anonymous peer feedback aggregated to prevent identification of individual responders. Written summaries reviewed by me before sharing with each person individually.

#### What We Found

**The positive surprises were the most impactful.**

I expected the useful feedback to be the constructive criticism — the gaps, the blind spots, the development areas. And those were valuable. But the feedback that changed behavior most immediately was the specific, named positive feedback that people had never heard directly.

One engineer didn't know that her peers specifically valued her for how she handled technical disagreements — presenting her position clearly, listening to alternative approaches, and updating her position gracefully when persuaded. She described this as "just how I talk." Her peers described it as "the reason our code reviews are collaborative instead of combative."

Knowing that a behavior you take for granted is specifically valued by your colleagues is a different kind of motivating signal than "you did a good job this quarter." It tells you what to keep doing, and it gives you a clear connection between a specific behavior and its impact on the people around you.

**The perception gaps were the most actionable data.**

The most useful output of a 360 process is the gap between self-perception and peer perception — cases where a person rates themselves significantly differently than their peers do.

We had both directions. One engineer rated himself consistently low on communication effectiveness. His peers rated him consistently high. In our 1:1 where I shared this, he said: "I always feel like I'm not explaining clearly enough." His teammates' experience: "He's the person I go to when I need to understand something complex." That gap — between felt inadequacy and actual impact — is something no amount of objective performance data would have surfaced.

We also had the reverse: a case where self-assessment and peer feedback diverged in the other direction. Handled carefully and privately, this created a productive development conversation that would have been much harder to start without the structured data.

**AI transformation creates evaluation uncertainty that 360 feedback doesn't solve — but it exposes.**

Leading a team through AI transformation means your performance benchmarks are changing faster than your review cycles. What does "senior AI engineer" performance look like? The definition from six months ago might not apply today.

Several team members' 360 results reflected this uncertainty directly. Peers weren't sure how to rate "technical depth" for someone who had strong traditional engineering skills but was still developing AI-specific knowledge. In retrospect, I should have explicitly addressed this in the questionnaire design.

What the process surfaced: a need for the team to collectively discuss what excellence looks like in an AI-augmented engineering environment. That conversation hadn't happened explicitly. The 360 process created the opening for it.

#### How I Shared the Results

I did not send people their summaries by email. I did not share them in a group setting.

Each team member received their summary in a scheduled 1:1. I had read their results in advance. I shared the highlights — positive feedback first, then development areas — with explicit context. "Three of your peers mentioned specifically that you do X, which I want to name because I don't think you know they see it that way." "One area where feedback was more mixed was Y — I've also noticed this, and I want to talk about what support might look like."

The preparation time for these conversations — reading each summary, preparing specific context for the conversation, thinking about what each person needed to hear and how they needed to hear it — took about four hours per person. Eleven people, four hours each, spread across two weeks.

It was the most valuable two weeks I've spent on team development in a long time.

#### What I'd Do Differently

The questionnaire was too long. Some of the dimensions I asked about overlapped with others, and the additional data didn't add proportional insight. I'd shorten it by about 30% next time.

I'd also build in a team-level debrief — not sharing individual results, but sharing themes that emerged across the team. There were several patterns that were worth naming at the team level (not attributable to individuals) that I kept private because I hadn't designed a safe way to surface them collectively.

And I'd do it more often. Annually minimum.

If you're leading a team and you've been waiting for a good time to do this: the good time is now. The results won't be comfortable. They shouldn't be. That's the point.
